## Purpose

This document defines the complete content creation workflow envisioned for PronounceLab Studio, from the first course idea to a learning experience that is ready for students.

The goal is to make lesson creation simple, consistent, and enjoyable for teachers. This document describes the intended teacher experience. It does not claim that every stage is currently available; complete student preview and end-to-end publication remain part of the product direction.

## Authoring Philosophy

Creating educational content should feel creative rather than technical. Teachers should spend their attention on learning goals, useful examples, meaningful practice, and the needs of their students instead of managing software.

The Studio should guide teachers naturally through every step. It should provide enough structure to support good decisions without limiting professional judgment. At each stage, the teacher should understand what they are creating, why it matters, and what to do next.

## Content Hierarchy

The authoring experience follows a clear educational hierarchy:

```text
Course
↓
Unit
↓
Lesson
↓
Activity
↓
Block
```

- **Course** defines a broad learning journey with a clear audience, purpose, and direction.
- **Unit** organizes related learning goals into a meaningful stage of that journey.
- **Lesson** delivers a focused learning experience that students can understand and complete.
- **Activity** gives students a specific way to observe, practise, apply, or reflect on learning.
- **Block** is the smallest reusable part of an activity, such as an instruction, example, prompt, question, or practice item.

Each level should have one clear responsibility. The hierarchy helps teachers move from broad curriculum intent to the details of learner action without losing context.

## Typical Workflow

### 1. Create a Course

The teacher begins by defining the overall learning journey. A course should have a clear purpose, intended audience, and direction so that later decisions remain connected to a shared goal.

### 2. Organize Units

Units divide the course into coherent stages. Each unit groups related goals and helps the teacher create a progression that students can follow without feeling overwhelmed.

### 3. Create Lessons

Each lesson turns part of a unit goal into a focused learning experience. The teacher defines what students should understand, practise, and be able to do by the end.

### 4. Add Activities

Activities shape the lesson flow. The teacher selects purposeful steps that move learners from introduction and observation toward guided practice, independent use, communication, and reflection.

### 5. Build Each Activity Using Reusable Blocks

Blocks give activities their content. Teachers combine clear instructions, examples, prompts, questions, and practice material while reusing suitable content and patterns instead of repeating avoidable work.

### 6. Review the Lesson

The teacher checks the complete learning sequence for clarity, accuracy, pace, and educational purpose. Review should confirm that every activity supports the lesson goal and that students receive enough guidance and practice.

### 7. Preview the Student Experience

The teacher experiences the lesson from the student's perspective. Preview helps reveal unclear instructions, weak transitions, unnecessary difficulty, missing context, and distractions that may not be obvious during authoring.

**Future:** A complete student-perspective preview is part of the intended workflow and should not be represented as fully available until the product provides it.

### 8. Publish

After review and preview, the teacher or responsible reviewer confirms that the lesson is complete and ready for students. Publication marks the end of preparation for that release, not the end of future improvement.

**Future:** The complete end-to-end publication experience remains a product direction. Current product documentation defines which release actions are available.

## Draft and Publication

The authoring lifecycle follows a simple principle:

```text
Draft → Review → Publish
```

- **Draft** is the creative workspace. Teachers should be able to explore ideas, change direction, test sequences, and improve content freely.
- **Review** is the quality stage. The lesson is checked as a complete learning experience rather than as a collection of separate parts.
- **Publish** expresses confidence that the lesson is ready for students.

Only reviewed lessons should become available to students. Drafting encourages experimentation; publication requires deliberate judgment. The transition between them should feel clear and meaningful.

## Editing Existing Lessons

Teaching materials improve through use, observation, and reflection. Teachers should be able to revisit lessons, strengthen explanations, refine practice, correct weaknesses, and respond to learner needs without fear of damaging an established student experience.

Continuous improvement is part of the authoring philosophy. Published learning should remain dependable while teachers prepare thoughtful improvements for a later release. The Studio should make that distinction easy to understand and support improvement as a normal part of teaching.

## Teacher Experience Principles

- **Every action should feel predictable.** Teachers should understand the result before they act.
- **The Studio should reduce cognitive load.** The product should present the right choices at the right moment.
- **Creating a lesson should require minimal clicks.** Common work should be direct without hiding important decisions.
- **Navigation should always be clear.** Teachers should know where they are within the course, unit, lesson, and activity.
- **Teachers should never feel lost.** The Studio should make the next useful step visible and make recovery easy.
- **The interface should encourage creativity rather than administration.** Structure should protect attention, not turn teaching into clerical work.

Teacher confidence is a requirement across the whole workflow. Speed matters, but clarity and trust matter more.

## Preview Philosophy

Previewing is essential because an authoring view cannot fully represent the student's experience. Teachers need to encounter the lesson's instructions, order, pacing, practice, and transitions as a learner would.

Preview is part of quality assurance, not an optional final decoration. It helps teachers verify that the lesson feels coherent, accessible, and complete before publication. A useful preview should encourage teachers to ask whether students will understand what to do and whether each step prepares them for the next.

## Publishing Philosophy

Publishing is not simply pressing a button. It is a professional decision that communicates confidence in the lesson.

When a teacher publishes, they should believe that the lesson has a clear goal, a complete learning flow, accurate and appropriate content, sufficient practice, and a meaningful outcome for students. Publication should feel deliberate without becoming burdensome.

## Future Evolution

The workflow may evolve as PronounceLab grows, teachers provide feedback, and new forms of learning become useful. Steps may become faster, guidance may improve, and authoring possibilities may expand.

The guiding principles should remain stable: protect teacher attention, keep the learning goal visible, make quality review natural, and preserve a clear path from idea to student experience. Growth should simplify meaningful work rather than add process for its own sake.

## Conclusion

The best authoring tools disappear into the background. PronounceLab Studio should allow teachers to concentrate entirely on teaching and learning, with the product quietly guiding the journey from an educational idea to a confident, complete learning experience.
